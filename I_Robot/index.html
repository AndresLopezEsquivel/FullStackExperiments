I, Robot

Before AI became reality, Asimov imagined its soul.

Reading I, Robot today feels remarkably different from reading it decades ago. When Isaac Asimov published these stories, intelligent machines belonged almost entirely to the realm of imagination. In 2026, however, we live in a world where artificial intelligence writes code, creates art, answers questions, and increasingly participates in decisions that affect millions of people. Because of this, I, Robot no longer feels like a distant vision of the future—it feels like a conversation about the present. What impresses me most about the book is that Asimov was not primarily interested in robots as machines. He was interested in the relationship between humans and technology. Many science-fiction stories portray robots as villains that inevitably rebel against humanity. Asimov chose a more sophisticated path. Through his famous Three Laws of Robotics, he explored what happens when intelligent systems are designed to help humans, yet still encounter situations where rules conflict with one another. This idea feels surprisingly relevant today. Modern AI systems are not governed by the Three Laws, but engineers face similar challenges. How do we ensure AI acts safely? How do we align machine behavior with human values? What happens when different objectives conflict? These are no longer philosophical questions reserved for science fiction; they are active areas of research and public debate. Another reason I, Robot remains powerful is that it highlights a truth often forgotten in discussions about AI: technology is rarely the only source of problems. In many of Asimov’s stories, difficulties emerge not because robots are malicious, but because humans misunderstand them, misuse them, or fail to anticipate the consequences of their own decisions. I believe this lesson is particularly important today. While there are legitimate concerns about AI, many risks come not from the technology itself but from how people deploy it, regulate it, and integrate it into society. The book also encourages readers to think critically about trust. As AI becomes more capable, people must decide when to rely on machines and when to question them. We already see this challenge with AI-generated content, automated decision systems, and autonomous technologies. Asimov understood that the future would not simply depend on creating intelligent machines; it would depend on developing a healthy relationship between human judgment and machine intelligence. Perhaps the most striking aspect of I, Robot is how optimistic it feels compared to much modern discourse. Asimov did not ignore risks, but he believed that science, reason, and careful engineering could help humanity navigate them. In an era where conversations about AI often swing between excitement and fear, this balanced perspective is refreshing. For me, I, Robot is not merely a collection of robot stories. It is a thoughtful exploration of ethics, responsibility, and the challenges that arise whenever humans create powerful new tools. More than seventy years after its publication, the book remains relevant because it asks questions that society is still trying to answer. As AI continues to transform our world, Asimov’s work reminds us that the most important question may not be what machines can do, but how humans choose to use them.
